CHAPTER II · PANTHEON

Deity Field Guide

From the Five Blessing Emperors to the Prince Squad
more than forty documented roles in the Fuzhou Youshen system

SCROLL

Fuzhou Youshen is one of the most complete and visually powerful deity-procession systems in coastal southeast China. Its pantheon brings together plague-expelling gods, Lushan ritual lineages, local protectors, historical heroes, underworld messengers, and newly beloved prince figures into a living ritual cast.

I

The Five Blessing Emperors

Core Plague-Expelling Deities

The theological foundation of Fuzhou Youshen

Origin legend and changing divine status

The Five Blessing Emperors, also called the Five Linggong, form one of the most important belief systems in Fuzhou. Their ancestral temple is Bailong An in Taijiang District.

"We shall give our lives to save the people." The five men drank the poisoned water and died, warning the whole city.

Their status changed in a crucial way: figures once linked to plague in Daoist cosmology were reimagined in Fuzhou as righteous men who sacrificed themselves for the people. In local belief, they transformed from bringers of pestilence into protectors who drive it away.

II

The Prince Squad

From Procession Atmosphere to Online Icons

Young divine relatives, viral faces, and a newly legible pantheon

The Chinese term shizi originally refers to the heir of a prince or noble lord. In Youshen, shizi figures are divine children or relatives created through ordinary human kinship language. They make the gods feel personal, familial, and emotionally approachable.

2024 Viral Icon

Prince Zhao

Son of Zhao Gongming, Zhenling Gong

Recognized by his side-parted hair, cool expression, black-and-gold dragon robe, and double-dragon feathered crown.

"A homebody, aloof, hard to invite out, said to have refused five years of divination requests."

III

Seventh and Eighth Lords

General Xie & General Fan

Among the oldest and most recognizable tǎgǔ figures

Their origin story

According to local legend, Xie Bi'an and Fan Wujiu were yamen runners from Min County in Fuzhou and sworn brothers. When a flood struck, Fan refused to leave their meeting place and drowned. Xie returned, found his brother dead, and hanged himself in grief. The King of Hell honored their loyalty by appointing them as ghost-catching messengers before the City God.

Seventh Lord · Xie Bi'an

White Impermanence · called "Tall Brother" in Fuzhou speech

He has a pale face with black bat patterns, a long tongue, and a tall hat often marked with auspicious words. His tǎgǔ frame is very tall, and the bearer looks out through an opening near the belly.

The name Xie Bi'an is popularly read as "thanks to the gods, peace will surely come."

Eighth Lord · Fan Wujiu

Black Impermanence · called "Short Eighth" in Fuzhou speech

He has a black face with white eyes, wears a round hat, and carries a plaque marked with moral judgment. His tǎgǔ frame is shorter, and the bearer looks out through the eyes.

The name Fan Wujiu is popularly read as "those who break the law cannot be saved."

IV

Eight Generals

The Underworld Judicial Team of the Five Emperors

Arrest, interrogation, punishment, and ritual order

The ritual process of arrest and judgment

The Eight Generals, sometimes expanded into the Ten Generals, originated in the Fuzhou cult of the Five Blessing Emperors. They serve as divine officers responsible for arresting ghosts, driving away evil, and carrying out ritual judgment.

The core group combines four major generals, Gan, Liu, Fan, and Xie, with the four seasonal gods of spring, summer, autumn, and winter. Their movement patterns include the Seven-Star Step, Eight-Trigram formation, and Four-Gate stamping.

V

Child Deity Roles

Hai'erdi, Nezha, and the Little Prince

Small bodies, quick movement, and youthful divine energy

Hai'erdi

The lively soul of the procession

Hai'erdi appears as a laughing child figure with a bellyband and small side braids. The performer inserts both arms into the sleeves, allowing the figure to dance with wild flexibility.

Taiwan's Electric-Techno Third Prince is historically connected to Fuzhou tǎgǔ child figures and Nezha performance.

Nezha

Central Altar Marshal · leader of the Five Camps

Nezha has a very high status in Fujian and Taiwan belief. Titles such as Great God of the Three Altars and Central Altar Marshal were strongly shaped by Fujian ritual culture.

He usually wears gold armor and red robes, holding the fire-tipped spear and cosmic ring while riding wind-fire wheels.

Little Prince

An entry point for child participation

About one meter tall, this figure is designed for children around ten years old to carry. In Fujian folk usage, "prince" often functions almost as another name for Nezha.

VI

Functional Roles

Horse Handler and Baozhang Gong

Processional order, humor, scouting, and local governance memory

Horse Handler · Opening Scout

Formerly called banmazai or scout

The horse handler wears black-and-white clothing, holds a whip, and is performed by a real person. He runs, turns, reports, and cracks the whip to clear away impurity and keep the route moving.

Being lightly touched by the horse handler's whip is believed to bring good luck in the coming year.

Baozhang Gong · Drunken Path Opener

A deified memory of the Qing baojia system

Baozhang Gong is humorous, tipsy, and unruly. He carries branches and swats at people blocking the road, turning local administrative memory into a playful ritual presence.

This is a distinctly Fuzhou role and is rarely found in other regions.

VII

Other Major Deities

Local Guardians, Monkey Gods, Fire Gods, and Martial Lords

Madam Linshui, Baima Zunwang, Qitian Dasheng, and more

Baima Zunwang

White Horse Third Lord

One tradition identifies him as the third son of the Min-Yue king Ying, who rode a white horse and slew an eel spirit. Another links him to the Min ruler Wang Shenzhi. In local practice, the two traditions have partly merged.

Qitian Dasheng

Monkey-god worship before Journey to the West

The ancestral temple is Zizhulin Dongtianfu on Pingshan in Fuzhou. The city is estimated to have more than 700 Great Sage shrines and altars. This belief was listed as Fujian provincial intangible cultural heritage in 2011.

Huaguang Dadi

Three-eyed Lord Ma · fire deity

Also known as Marshal Ma, a Daoist protector and one of the Four Sacred Guardians. His third eye is his key visual marker. He is worshiped by groups such as shed builders and ceramic workers.

Zhao Tianjun

A local deity of Fuzhou's southern gate area

One story says a mysterious firelight appeared beside an old banyan tree to illuminate a dangerous road. Another links him to Zhao Tianzhu of the Tang dynasty, who hung red lanterns from a banyan tree to warn the people.

Yinglie Wang Jiushi Gong

The supreme main deity of Houfu Youshen

His main temple is Yingwu Temple. During the late Ming period, a flood destroyed the original temple and the deity image drifted to Houfu Village, where a new worship tradition began.

Guan Di

A protective cover image during bans on the Five Emperors

In Fuzhou belief, Guan Di often served as a safer public face when worship of the Five Blessing Emperors was suppressed. Youshen tǎgǔ also includes the figure of Prince Guan Ping.

VIII

Generals Wen and Kang

The Jiuyan Shisantang System

A major traditional worship circle in urban Fuzhou

The Jiuyan Shisantang system

Jiuyan Shisantang is a major traditional worship circle around the border of Taijiang and Gulou districts in Fuzhou. Its core deities are Generals Wen and Kang under Dongyue Dadi, together honored as the Nine-Thousand-Year Lords.

The system began in the late Ming and early Qing period. The area was low-lying and full of ponds, known as the Eighteen Yang. During a major epidemic, Taipingshan Village suffered less than surrounding villages, leading thirteen villages to form an alliance around shared worship.

The Jiuyan Taishan Youshen lasts about half a month and is the largest urban Youshen event in Fuzhou. It is listed as district-level intangible cultural heritage in Taijiang.

Marshal Wen · Wen Qiong

Wen Taibao of Jiuyan Shisantang

After swallowing a thousand plague pills, his face turned blue and he became a fierce blue-faced, red-haired divine general. He is also one of the Daoist Four Sacred Guardians.

Marshal Kang · Kang Xi

Yinglie Wang of Jiuyan Shisantang

Known for compassion and refusal to kill. In legend, he saved an injured crane, and the crane repaid him with longevity grass. The Jade Emperor granted him the title Benevolent Sacred Marshal.

IX

Other Deities and Roles

Local Gods and Additional Tǎgǔ Figures

A broader field of Fuzhou-specific ritual images

Fuzhou City God

Fuzhou has a provincial City God Temple, and Qing sources record City God procession activities.

Marshal Tiandu

A deified form of Lei Haiqing, a loyal Tang musician. His forehead is marked by a crab emblem.

Mazu

Fuzhou has Tianhou temples, but local deities such as the Five Blessing Emperors and Madam Linshui are especially prominent in the city.

Master Pei

A Fuzhou-specific figure. His original name is linked to a written transformation in which "fei" and "yi" combine into the character Pei.

Sizhou Dasheng

Originally associated with the Tang monk Sengjia. The belief took root in Fuzhou through officials from Sizhou who served in the region.

Chen Wenlong

A Southern Song general who resisted the Yuan. The Ming court honored him as Minister of Water and later as King Who Pacifies the Sea.

Zhang Sheng Zhenjun

A deity associated with Fujian merchant communities, symbolizing solidarity and collective effort.

Civil Top Scholar

A civil-official type tǎgǔ figure that appears behind the Prince Squad in the procession order.

How tǎgǔ figures are made

The head cylinder is carved from camphor wood. The process may include sourcing the wood, resting it for about a year, rough cutting, hollowing, sanding, hand-carving the face, base painting, multiple lacquer layers, eyebrow setting, and final color painting. The body frame is woven from bamboo strips. A tǎgǔ figure usually weighs around 30 to 80 jin, and larger ones can reach about three meters tall.

In recent years, some new Prince Squad designs have also used 3D printing, combining bamboo weaving with Fuzhou's bodiless lacquerware tradition and contemporary visual culture.

A Living Museum of Deities

The Fuzhou Youshen pantheon is not a static list of names. It is a living religious ecology. From the Five Pestilence Spirits in early Daoist texts to contemporary prince figures shaped by young designers, every role carries layers from different historical moments.

What matters most is the system's capacity to adapt. It can turn plague gods into protectors, transform a local administrative officer into a path-opening ritual figure, and allow 3D printing, anime-like aesthetics, bamboo weaving, and centuries of camphor-wood carving to coexist.

"Every new Youshen season may bring a new tǎgǔ figure into the procession."

When young people find emotional connection through stories of a homebody Prince Zhao or a candy-loving Prince Huaguang, Youshen completes its latest act of renewal.
