CHAPTER I · ORIGINS

Three Millennia

From Fangxiangshi to viral deity processions
a long ritual genealogy of Fuzhou Youshen

SCROLL

Fuzhou Youshen is not a single invented festival. It is a layered ritual system formed through ancient exorcistic rites, Buddhist image processions, city-god patrols, Min-Yue shamanic traditions, temple networks, and the social life of local communities.

I

Shang to Han

The Nuo Ritual & Fangxiangshi Prototype

The early exorcistic model behind masked, weapon-bearing ritual guardians

Fangxiangshi: a distant ancestor of the processional guard

The Rites of Zhou describes Fangxiangshi as a ritual officer who wore a bearskin, had four golden eyes, dressed in dark upper garments and red lower garments, and carried a spear and shield to drive away harmful forces.

"Fangxiangshi wore bearskin, bore four golden eyes, held spear and shield, and led attendants in seasonal exorcisms to search the chambers and expel pestilence."

This figure offers an early model for later guardian images: frightening features, enlarged bodies, weapons, and movement through space. In Fuzhou Youshen, the towering tǎgǔ figures carried by human bearers continue that old grammar of protection in a local form.

The Great Nuo of the Han dynasty

"On the day before the twelfth-month sacrifice, the Great Nuo was performed to expel disease. Boys were chosen as ritual attendants; Fangxiangshi appeared with four golden eyes, bearskin, dark garments, red skirt, spear, and shield."

The Han ritual involved chanting, dancing, torches, and a final act of sending pestilence outside the gate. Three ideas are especially relevant to later Fuzhou practice: masked exorcistic bodies, movement through a defined space, and the removal of danger beyond the community boundary.

From court ritual to local practice

The Analects records that when villagers performed Nuo, Confucius stood in court dress at the eastern steps. This small detail shows that exorcistic performance was not limited to the palace; it also existed in local society.

Over time, court rites moved downward, local officials imitated state ceremonies, and southern shamanic traditions gave Nuo culture a strong regional base. These processes helped ritual protection become part of everyday community life.

II

Min-Yue Shamanism

A Regional Genealogy of Spirits and Mediums

The deep local background of Fujian's spirit-centered religious culture

"The Yue people honored spirits"

The Min-Yue kingdom, centered around Dongye in present-day Fuzhou, was known for a strong culture of spirit worship and shamanic practice. Han historical records describe Yue ritual specialists whose methods, including divination, were later adopted at the imperial court.

"The Yue people were accustomed to spirits, and their sacrifices often produced visible signs." — paraphrased from the Book of Han, Treatise on Sacrifices

This matters because Fuzhou Youshen did not grow in an empty cultural space. It developed in a region where communication with spirits, ritual specialists, and local sacred power already had deep roots.

A kingdom ended, but the culture remained

In 110 BCE, Emperor Wu of Han defeated Min-Yue and relocated part of its population. Yet the culture did not simply disappear. Many local groups remained in the mountains and valleys, while later Han migrants brought new institutions, writing systems, and ritual forms.

The result was not replacement, but long-term layering: Min-Yue shamanic traditions and Central Plains ritual culture gradually merged into a distinctive Fujian religious ecology.

Archaeological traces

The Chengcun Han city site at Wuyi Mountain provides important material evidence for Min-Yue culture. Finds such as snake-patterned bricks and ritual objects echo older textual associations between Fujian, Yue peoples, and serpent symbolism.

The boat-shaped hanging coffins of Wuyi Mountain also point to a religious imagination concerned with the afterlife, elevation, and the journey of the soul.

III

Buddhist Image Processions

The Ritual Template for Moving Sacred Images

From embodied exorcism to images carried through public space

Faxian and Xuanzang: first-hand accounts

A key transformation in the history of processional religion was the shift from a person dressing as a divine or exorcistic figure to a sacred image being carried or wheeled in public. Buddhist image processions offered a powerful model for that shift.

"Three or four li outside the city, a four-wheeled image carriage was made, more than three zhang high, shaped like a moving palace." — Faxian, A Record of Buddhist Kingdoms

Xuanzang also described monasteries decorating Buddha images with jewels and brocade, placing them on carriages, and moving them in large public gatherings. The image left the hall and became a public presence.

Luoyang: image processions in a Chinese urban setting

"A six-tusked white elephant carried the image of Sakyamuni; lions led the way, and performers swallowed knives, breathed fire, and filled the city with extraordinary spectacle." — paraphrased from A Record of Buddhist Monasteries in Luoyang

The parallels with later Youshen are clear: animal and guardian figures lead the way; sacred images move through public streets; banners, music, and performers surround the deity. Buddhist image processions supplied a kind of ritual operating system for later community deity processions.

IV

Min Kingdom to Song

The Formation of Fujian's Religious Ecology

Local rulers, Buddhist institutions, city gods, and popular belief

Wang Shenzhi and the joining of Buddhism with local belief

Wang Shenzhi, founder of the Min Kingdom, strongly supported Buddhism during his rule. He is said to have built and repaired 266 Buddhist temples and pagodas, helping Fujian become one of the most active Buddhist regions in China.

"Spectators stood like walls; Buddhist chants rose to the sky; banners and flowers lit the whole city."

After his death, Wang Shenzhi was deified as Baima Zunwang and Kaimin Shengwang. This transformation of a historical ruler into a worshipped deity became one model for the rich person-deity tradition later visible in Fuzhou Youshen.

The institutionalization of City God processions

The Song dynasty was a crucial period in the transformation from Buddhist image processions to popular deity festivals. Fuzhou's City God Temple can be traced back to the Jin dynasty, making it one of the earliest City God temples in China.

City God processions, held three times a year, already included image carrying, ritual guards, music, street performance, and public movement. In form, they were very close to later Youshen processions.

"In Fujian, people turned more readily to shamans than to physicians when illness entered the home." — paraphrased from Cai Xiang
V

The Five Emperors Set Sail

1642 and the Fuzhou Genesis of Youshen

A plague story, a salvation legend, and the earliest large-scale record

The Five Blessing Emperors: legend and historical memory

The Five Blessing Emperors, also known as the Five Linggong, are central to Fuzhou Youshen. A local legend says that during the late Ming period, five examination candidates discovered a divine order to poison five wells during a plague. To warn the city, they each threw themselves into a well.

"We shall give our lives to save the people." — attributed in Min Zaji

Rongcheng Jiwen: the earliest large-scale record of Fuzhou Youshen

"In 1642, plague broke out in Fuzhou. Local communities gathered money, set up ritual offerings, and performed Great Nuo for the Five Emperors. Some people painted ghostly faces or acted as underworld attendants, parading through the streets. One village finished, another began; from the second to the eighth lunar month, towns and villages seemed to become a realm of spirits." — paraphrased from Rongcheng Jiwen

This passage is important because it clearly names the ritual as Great Nuo, records face painting and attendant roles, and describes a citywide scale. By 1642, Youshen was not an isolated event; it had become a broad social movement across Fuzhou.

VI

Three Qing Centuries

Official Prohibition and Local Persistence

The repeated struggle between state discipline and community ritual

Three bans and a tradition that would not vanish

Qing regulations against Fuzhou deity processions repeatedly complained that prohibitions had failed. That language is revealing: it shows how deeply Youshen was rooted in local society.

In 1700, a Fuzhou prefect demolished temples of the Five Emperors to build official school facilities. After he left office, the temples not only returned; their number grew to more than ten.

"Banners and ritual guards filled the road; even officials did not make the procession yield." — paraphrased from Zha Shenxing

Adaptive strategies under pressure

🏯

Borrowing the name of Guandi

Some temples presented the Five Emperors as figures associated with Guandi and renamed themselves Martial Saint temples.

🏛

Becoming an "an"

Other sites adopted names associated with Guanyin halls, using a more acceptable Buddhist identity as cover.

✨

Renaming as Wuxian

The Five Emperors were sometimes blended with the legally safer cult of the Five Manifest Lords.

🔄

Shifting the main deity

Temples could place a supporting deity, such as Lord Ma, in the official position of main deity.

These tactics helped produce Fuzhou's distinctive temple naming system, often remembered through the phrase "nine an and eleven jian."

Justus Doolittle: a 19th-century foreign witness

Justus Doolittle arrived in Fuzhou in 1850 and spent fourteen years in Fujian. His two-volume Social Life of the Chinese, published in 1865, remains one of the most detailed English-language accounts of everyday Chinese life in the mid-19th century.

"When an official's sedan chair met a procession for the Five Emperors, the procession did not give way; instead, it demanded that the official retreat or move aside so the deity sedan could pass." — paraphrased from Justus Doolittle, Social Life of the Chinese
VII

Rupture and Revival

From Prohibition to Billions of Views

A broken tradition re-enters public life and digital circulation

A century of rupture

In the Republican period, Youshen was targeted under anti-superstition campaigns. After 1949, it was officially labeled feudal superstition and systematically banned. During the Cultural Revolution, the destruction of the Four Olds brought severe damage to temples, images, and ritual memory.

Yet preservation also happened quietly. In Fuzhou, cultural workers covered temple inscriptions with lime and political slogans, protecting them for decades under a surface that looked ideologically safe.

1992: the first restored collective procession

In 1992, after decades of interruption, the Jiuyan Shisantang communities held their first restored collective procession.

"There were no streetlights at night, so people carried kerosene lamps. Whenever the procession reached a village, residents knelt along the road, welcomed the deities, set out banquets, lit firecrackers, and beat gongs and drums."

2024: from local ritual to national phenomenon

During the 2024 Lunar New Year, Fuzhou Youshen exploded across short-video platforms. In Houfu, more than 400 deity figures were invited out, and 17 natural villages joined the procession.

VIII

Converging Origins

A Scholarly Debate on Where Youshen Comes From

No single source explains the whole tradition

Four major explanations

01

The Nuo Ritual Theory

This view sees Youshen as an inheritance of ancient exorcistic rites. Its key evidence is the link between masked Nuo figures, enlarged tǎgǔ bodies, and the function of expelling disease.

02

The Image Procession Theory

This view argues that Buddhist image processions provided the ritual template for carrying sacred images through public streets with music, banners, attendants, and performance.

03

The City God Theory

This explanation locates Youshen's direct predecessor in Song-dynasty City God patrols, which help explain its official tone, public order, and processional organization.

04

The Convergence Theory

This is the strongest overall explanation: Nuo supplied the exorcistic logic, Buddhist processions supplied the moving-image format, Daoist ritual supplied liturgical structure, and popular religion supplied the social base.

IX

Why Fuzhou?

A Ritual Greenhouse in a River Basin

Geography, temples, migration, overseas wealth, and local autonomy

⛰

Geographic enclosure

Fuzhou sits in the lower Min River basin, surrounded by hills and mountains. This landscape helped keep local communities relatively distant from central power and encouraged temple-based self-organization.

⚓

Maritime and diaspora wealth

Large processions can cost hundreds of thousands or even millions of yuan. In Changle, a famous hometown of overseas Chinese, remittances and diaspora support became an important financial base.

🏘

The jìng community system

In Fuzhou, local temple territories are often called jìng. Each jìng worships specific guardian deities and organizes ritual, mutual aid, local order, and community identity.

🐍

Long Min-Yue continuity

From ancient spirit worship to 21st-century digital platforms, Fujian's tendency to center local gods, ritual specialists, and temple networks has shown remarkable durability.

Why Ritual Outlasts Prohibition

The long history of Fuzhou Youshen points to one central lesson: a ritual survives because of what it does for a community, not because authority always approves of it.

Youshen is not the fossil of one ancient rite. It is an open system that has absorbed exorcistic protection, Buddhist image movement, City God patrols, Daoist liturgy, diaspora funding, craft innovation, youth aesthetics, and digital circulation.

"The post-80s fund it, the post-90s manage it, and the post-00s carry it."

In the end, Fuzhou Youshen is a story of cultural resilience: a community-based ritual system repeatedly damaged by state power, urban change, globalization, and media transformation, yet able to return because it still makes the community visible, connected, and protected.
